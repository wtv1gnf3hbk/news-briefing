Good morning. Here's the state of play:

Iran's chokehold on global oil supplies is getting tighter. After weeks of escalating conflict, Tehran has effectively [strangled the Strait](https://www.nytimes.com/2026/03/12/us/politics/strait-of-hormuz-iran-war.html) of Hormuz, the narrow waterway that carries about a fifth of the world's oil. It's a classic asymmetric response from a weakened regime: when you can't match your opponents militarily, you target what hurts most. Oil prices have spiked above $100 a barrel, and the economic shockwaves are already rippling through everything from mortgage rates to global supply chains.

The human toll keeps mounting too. Four American crew members died when their [refueling plane crashed](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/cy0dz5ql17vo) in Iraq, according to the BBC. Defense Secretary Hegseth claims Iran's Supreme Leader Khamenei is "likely disfigured" from recent strikes, but Tehran showed it can still project power by launching [new attacks](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/liveblog/2026/3/13/iran-war-live-trump-says-war-going-well-as-gulf-under-wave-of-attacks) on various targets, Al Jazeera reports. Meanwhile, NATO defenses in Turkey intercepted a third Iranian missile.

• European stocks managed to recover after oil prices dipped back [below $100](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-13/european-stocks-set-for-second-weekly-drop-as-iran-war-weighs) a barrel, according to Bloomberg, but it's been a volatile week for markets trying to price in an unpredictable conflict.
